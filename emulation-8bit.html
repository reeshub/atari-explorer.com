For those of you wishing to revive those days of old, or if those days passed you by completely, you can now emulate the Atari 8-Bit series on your PC desktop.  Simply download the emulator and the ROM files (located on the side menu) and un-zip them to a location on your hard drive.  For further instructions on setting-up the emulator and playing the games available here, please consult the instructions provided with the emulator, or click the Quick-Start icon below.

Also, on the side menu, we have a number of "History Files" for some of the games.  Only our favourite games get this treatment, and you are always welcome to contribute if you can.

After the original Breakout by Atari, "bat and ball" games took a break (out!) for a while.  It wasn't until Taito, the Japanese arcade company launched Arkanoid in the mid eighties, that the craze returned.   Arkanoid was converted to all the popular home computer formats, and at a time when the Atari 8-Bit was going through a drought of mainstream titles, Imagine decided there was room for an Atari version - and a competent one at that.

 ©1987 Imagine/Taito

Atari initiated the first of a series of game titles with Lucasfilm in the early 80's.  They were designed to push the Atari 400/800 as far as possible, and design new original concepts in game design through the imagination of the Lucasfilm company.  Ballblazer is one of the finest 2-Player games written for the 8-Bit series, one of the few titles that has never been successfully translated to any other platform, as the Playstation version demonstrates.

© 1984 Lucasfilm Games/Atari/Epyx

Druid was another title that made it to the Atari 8-Bit series after its success on the other major home computers of the mid to late 80's.  It is one of the few "full-price" titles from Firebird for the Atari, as the majority of their products were launched on budget labels.  An enjoyable game with spells and potions, and a slight "Gauntlet" theme.

© Firebird Software (B.T. Carter original version)

Dropzone by Archer Mclean (International Karate etc)  was launched under the US Gold label in 1984.  A "defender-like" game, it is a fast and furious combination of rescue and blasting that showed off the scrolling and colour capabilities of the Atari 8-Bit, especially to your Commodore 64 friends!  The game was an instant hit, and played even better than than the official computer port of Defender by Atari.

©1985 U.S. Gold (Archer Mclean)

A driving game with a difference, with various obstacles  hindering  your progress to the next level.  The graphics were incredible and the game was very fast on the 1.79 MHz Atari. This was a race against time, and the music actually complimented this with super graphics, a great soundtrack and another fine title from the English Software Company.  Adam Billyard who wrote this game, also wrote the weird "3D Pool" title for the Atari ST.

 ©1986 English Software Company (Adam Billyard)

Novagen software produced few games, but when they did you knew it would be special.  From the makers of the classic Mercenary series, Encounter is a great blaster, and so much better than the Commodore 64 version.  This is one of the few games that utilise the real speed of the Atari 8-Bit computer, and compared to the pedestrian speed of its older brother Battlezone (Atari), this version is a much more frantic shoot 'em up.

 ©1984 Novagen (Paul Woakes)

I remember waiting for this one!  A real classic computer version of the film and one of the few Atari 8-Bit games to include speech that was actually recognisable!  A catchy, if simple soundtrack, but a great game!  With the craze and hype surrounded by the feature film, and the hit track playing on the radio all year, Activision made a clever move by picking up the home computer rights to this game.  Great gameplay and another Activision hit.

 ©1985 Activision (David Crane)

Just like so many games of the time, Atari 8-Bit users were "lucky" to get this one!  Head Over Heels is a cute platform game using an isometric viewpoint.  A smash hit on the other home computer platforms, it involves two cute characters which you control through a series of brain teasing challenges.  This was at the height of the isometric craze, and similar games on the Atari, such as Chimera and Molecule Man shared the viewpoint.

 © 1988 Ocean Software (Jon Ritman and Bernie Drummond)

I could play this for hours!  Jet Boot Jack is simply a great platform game, simply collect all the musical notes to clear the level...  What makes this game so addictive is that on first inspection the screens look so easy to clear, but they clearly aren't!  This gives the game, which is such a beautifully simple concept, an incredible "just one more go" quality that few games can claim.  Thanks to Jon Williams for giving this to us!

 © 1983 English Software Company  (Jon Williams)

Novagen made their name with Mercenary.  Author Paul Woakes stunned the world with this title, and it has to be seen to be believed.  Super fast vector 3D graphics in a game-world with no rules.  Fly or drive around and take in the amazing scenery, or, as is intended, become a mercenary!  Definitely the best 8-bit version available.  If you get stuck on this one, we have the entire map available in the Mercenary History file.

 © 1985 Novagen (Paul Woakes)

Pacman... I don't think this game needs any introduction.  A nice conversion to the Atari home computer.

© 1982 Atari Inc. (Temporarily unavailable)

Another Atari/Lucasfilm game, and one of my all time favourites.  Pilot your spaceship to the planet surface and rescue your fellow pilots.  Amazing fractal scenery, combined with night flying and the odd Alien...  Just don't let them into the airlock!  What makes this game unique (apart from the amazing fractal scenery routines), is the actual feeling you get when rescuing a downed pilot, once he knocks on the airlock door, you heart begins to beat...

 © 1984 Lucasfilm Games/Atari/Epyx (David Fox)

Possibly the best space game ever on the Atari 8-bit, if you like to shoot things, with a pinch of strategy, this is the game for you.  When I first got this game in 1985, all I did was shoot asteroids for the first hour, and even that was fun!  This was the closest you could get to  flying a real spaceship and making a real difference in this solar system, we call home ;-)

© 1983 Atari Inc. (Doug Neubauer)
